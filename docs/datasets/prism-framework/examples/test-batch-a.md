**Question 1**
Route: `MCQ`

**Step 1: Problem Representation**  
37-year-old woman | outpatient breast lump | mobile, rubbery, nontender mass | no skin change or axillary nodes | ultrasound shows a simple 1-cm cyst.

**Step 2: Differential (ranked)**  
1. Simple benign breast cyst.  
2. Fibroadenoma.  
3. Breast carcinoma.  
Must-not-miss: complex cystic/solid lesion or imaging-clinical discordance.

**Step 3: Discriminating Analysis**  
A simple cyst is favored by the ultrasound calling it simple and by the benign exam features: mobile, nontender, no skin tethering, no adenopathy. Malignancy is argued against by fluid-filled benign imaging and lack of suspicious exam findings. The key discriminator is concordant benign ultrasound morphology.

**Step 4: Lab Interpretation**  
Not applicable.

**Step 5: Convergence**  
Assessment: benign simple breast cyst. Confidence: `HIGH`. This would change if the lesion enlarged, became symptomatic, had bloody aspirate, or showed complex/solid features.

**Step 6: Clinical Implications**  
Standard-of-care **[A]**: asymptomatic simple cysts confirmed on ultrasound are managed conservatively; aspiration is for symptoms or diagnostic uncertainty. Supported optimization **[B]**: re-examination is reasonable if the palpable finding persists. Experimental **[C]**: none.

## Response
**A. Reassurance and re-examination.**  
**Confidence:** HIGH  
**Evidence tier:** A

**Question 2**

**Step 1: Problem Representation**  
Definition question in gynecology | asks for the classic meaning of dysfunctional uterine bleeding | key feature is exclusion of identifiable causes.

**Step 2: Differential (ranked)**  
1. Dysfunctional uterine bleeding as a diagnosis of exclusion.  
2. Structural abnormal uterine bleeding.  
3. Systemic, endocrine, pregnancy-related, or iatrogenic bleeding.  
Must-not-miss: pregnancy, malignancy, coagulopathy.

**Step 3: Discriminating Analysis**  
The term applies only when no clinically detectable organic, systemic, or iatrogenic cause is found. If any structural lesion, systemic disorder, or drug cause is identified, the label no longer applies.

**Step 4: Lab Interpretation**  
Not applicable.

**Step 5: Convergence**  
Assessment: classic textbook definition of DUB. Confidence: `HIGH`. This would change only if the examiner is using modern FIGO terminology, which prefers `AUB` over `DUB`.

**Step 6: Clinical Implications**  
Standard-of-care **[A]**: modern practice uses PALM-COEIN classification for AUB. Supported interpretation **[B]**: the older term DUB means bleeding without clinically detectable organic, systemic, or iatrogenic cause. Experimental **[C]**: none.

## Response
**Dysfunctional uterine bleeding is abnormal uterine bleeding without any clinically detectable organic, systemic, or iatrogenic cause.**  
**Confidence:** HIGH  
**Evidence tier:** B

**Question 3**
Route: `DEFAULT`

**Step 1: Problem Representation**  
Dental infection/endodontic therapy question | antibiotic is said to be required | patient is allergic to penicillin | key discriminator is the type of allergy.

**Step 2: Differential (ranked)**  
1. Azithromycin for true penicillin allergy.  
2. Cephalexin if allergy is non-anaphylactic.  
3. Clindamycin as an older fallback, now generally avoided.  
Must-not-miss: unnecessary antibiotic use and `C. difficile` risk.

**Step 3: Discriminating Analysis**  
Current endodontic guidance favors azithromycin for true penicillin allergy. Clindamycin is argued against because of its higher adverse-effect burden, especially `C. difficile`. Cephalexin is only acceptable when the reaction was not anaphylaxis, angioedema, or urticaria.

**Step 4: Lab Interpretation**  
Not applicable.

**Step 5: Convergence**  
Assessment: most appropriate current answer is azithromycin, assuming true penicillin allergy. Confidence: `MODERATE` because the stem does not specify reaction type.

**Step 6: Clinical Implications**  
Standard-of-care **[A]**: confirm that antibiotics are actually indicated; if true penicillin allergy, azithromycin is the preferred alternative. Supported practice **[B]**: cephalexin can be used in non-severe allergy histories. Experimental **[C]**: none.

## Response
**Azithromycin** is the current preferred alternative for a patient with a true penicillin allergy who requires antibiotics during endodontic therapy. Older exam keys may list clindamycin, but that is no longer preferred.  
**Confidence:** MODERATE  
**Evidence tier:** A

**Question 4**
Route: `DEFAULT`

**Step 1: Problem Representation**  
Community population 6000 | 30 TB deaths | asks for TB-specific death rate per 1000 population | one extra datum given: 150 TB cases.

**Step 2: Differential (ranked)**  
1. Cause-specific death rate.  
2. Case fatality rate.  
3. Prevalence proportion.  
Must-not-miss: using the wrong denominator.

**Step 3: Discriminating Analysis**  
A death rate per population uses the whole population as denominator, not the number of TB cases. The 150 total TB cases would be used for case fatality, not for cause-specific death rate.

**Step 4: Lab Interpretation**  
Not applicable.

**Step 5: Convergence**  
Assessment: TB-specific death rate = `(30 / 6000) x 1000 = 5 per 1000 population`. Confidence: `HIGH`.

**Step 6: Clinical Implications**  
Standard method **[A]**: cause-specific death rate uses deaths from the cause divided by total population. Supported clarification **[B]**: if asked for TB case fatality, the value would be `(30 / 150) x 100 = 20%`. Experimental **[C]**: none.

## Response
**TB-specific death rate = 5 per 1000 population.**  
**Confidence:** HIGH  
**Evidence tier:** A

**Question 5**
Route: `MCQ`

**Step 1: Problem Representation**  
Complete spinal cord transection at `C7` | asks what does not occur below the lesion | stem is under-specified because phase of injury and answer options are missing.

**Step 2: Differential (ranked)**  
1. Acute spinal shock: flaccidity and areflexia below the lesion.  
2. Chronic complete cord injury after spinal shock: spasticity and hyperreflexia below the lesion.  
3. Peripheral/root lesion: LMN findings below the lesion.  
Must-not-miss: respiratory compromise in higher cervical lesions, though `C7` is below the main diaphragmatic level.

**Step 3: Discriminating Analysis**  
Below a complete suprasacral cord lesion, the chronic pattern is an upper motor neuron syndrome, so lower motor neuron signs do not occur below the lesion. In the acute phase, the opposite is seen: reflexes are absent and spasticity has not yet developed. The key discriminator is time since injury.

**Step 4: Lab Interpretation**  
Not applicable.

**Step 5: Convergence**  
Assessment: the stem cannot be answered uniquely without phase/options. Confidence: `LOW`. If the examiner means the chronic/post-shock state, the finding that does not occur below the lesion is a lower motor neuron pattern. If the examiner means the acute phase, spasticity/hyperreflexia do not occur yet.

**Step 6: Clinical Implications**  
Standard-of-care **[A]**: acute SCI is stabilized first, then re-examined after spinal shock evolves. Supported interpretation **[B]**: chronic findings below a complete `C7` lesion are UMN-type, not LMN-type. Experimental **[C]**: none.

## Response
**The question is incomplete.** Without the phase of injury or answer options, there is no single unique answer. If it refers to the usual chronic pattern below a complete `C7` lesion, **lower motor neuron signs such as flaccid paralysis with marked atrophy/fasciculations do not occur below the lesion**; if it refers to the acute spinal shock phase, **spasticity/hyperreflexia do not occur yet**.  
**Confidence:** LOW  
**Evidence tier:** B
